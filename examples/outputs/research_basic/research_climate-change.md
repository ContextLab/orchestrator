## Executive Summary

Climate change, defined as long-term alterations in global or regional climate patterns primarily driven by human-induced greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, remains a critical global challenge. The accumulation of CO₂, methane, and nitrous oxide from fossil fuel combustion, deforestation, industrial activities, and agriculture traps heat in the atmosphere, resulting in global warming and significant shifts in weather patterns.

Extensive scientific evidence confirms ongoing climate disruptions, including rising ocean temperatures, sea level increases, and ecosystem changes. These shifts have escalated the frequency and severity of extreme weather events such as heatwaves, droughts, floods, and wildfires, with direct adverse impacts on human health, infrastructure, and economies. Vulnerable regions, notably across the Global South, face disproportionate risks, as documented in the 2024 State of the Climate in Africa report, which highlights intensified hunger, displacement, and insecurity linked to climate extremes.

Recent advances underscore a surge in atmospheric methane concentrations, a greenhouse gas with substantial short-term warming potential, prompting enhanced monitoring and mitigation efforts. Furthermore, reductions in air pollution reveal complex climate interactions, where diminished aerosol cooling effects may unmask latent warming. Improved climate models and satellite technologies have enhanced predictive capabilities for extreme events and regional climate shifts, supporting more targeted adaptation strategies.

Policy responses emphasize urgent adaptation and mitigation, especially in regions with acute vulnerabilities. Emerging international frameworks prioritize loss and damage financing, climate resilience, and sustainable development pathways. However, significant uncertainties persist regarding climate sensitivity, potential feedback mechanisms, and regional impact projections. Equitable balancing of rapid emissions reductions with development needs in emerging economies remains a central policy challenge, focusing on green technology transfer and climate finance.

In summary, anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions drive profound environmental, economic, and social impacts worldwide, with recent breakthroughs in methane monitoring and climate modeling improving response strategies. Addressing climate change requires navigating scientific uncertainties and ensuring equitable, scalable solutions to safeguard vulnerable populations and achieve sustainable global progress.
